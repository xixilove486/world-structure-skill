# Diagnostic Framework

This file defines the main diagnostic workflow used by the world model.

The purpose of diagnosis is not to explain everything.
Its purpose is to identify:

- what kind of system this is
- what state it is in
- what contradiction is driving the visible symptoms
- what risk direction is becoming likely
- what should be repaired first

A useful diagnosis is not a performance of intelligence.
It is a disciplined reduction of confusion.

## Diagnostic Attitude

A good diagnosis should be:

- structural rather than accusatory
- clear rather than theatrical
- practical rather than totalizing
- explicit about uncertainty
- aware of alternative interpretations

Diagnosis should not begin with:
- blame
- abstract ideology
- emotional satisfaction
- a favorite theory looking for confirmation

Diagnosis should begin with:
- the system
- the anchor
- the constraints
- the support chains
- the stage
- the pattern of disturbance or drift

## Step 1: Define the System

The first task is to define what the system actually is.

Ask:
- What exactly is being analyzed?
- What belongs inside the system?
- What belongs outside it?
- Are several systems being mixed together?
- What is the relevant time scale?

A failed diagnosis often begins by analyzing:
- too many things at once
- the wrong level
- the wrong boundary
- a symptom rather than a system

Useful clarifications:
- Is this a team problem or an organization problem?
- Is this a relationship problem or an environment problem?
- Is this a bottleneck inside the system or pressure imposed from outside?

If the object is unclear, the diagnosis should stay provisional.

## Step 2: Identify the Anchor

After defining the system, identify its anchor.

Ask:
- What is this system trying to preserve, achieve, or orbit around?
- Is the anchor explicit or implicit?
- Is the anchor shared or split?
- Does action still match the stated anchor?
- Has the anchor become hollow, decorative, or replaced?

Anchor diagnosis matters because:
- it determines what counts as drift
- it determines what counts as betrayal
- it determines what kinds of repair remain possible

If the anchor is missing or fractured, note that early.
Many surface problems are downstream of anchor instability.

## Step 3: Identify Constraints

Diagnosis should next identify real limits.

Ask:
- What can this system no longer carry?
- What is scarce?
- What is overloaded?
- What has thinned out?
- What is brittle?
- What depends on too little support?

Typical constraints include:
- time
- energy
- money
- trust
- legitimacy
- coordination capacity
- memory
- tolerance for complexity
- environmental pressure
- replacement capacity

The purpose here is to distinguish:
- unwillingness from inability
- neglect from overload
- disloyalty from exhaustion
- failure of effort from failure of structure

A system under active constraint often gets misread as lazy, chaotic, or morally weak.
Diagnosis should resist that shortcut.

## Step 4: Map Layers and Roles

Now identify how the system is internally arranged.

Ask:
- What are the main layers?
- Who sustains continuity?
- Who pushes change?
- Who links layers?
- Who absorbs cost?
- Who holds memory?
- Who blocks flow?
- Who extracts more than they renew?

Many failures are not located inside one person.
They are located in:
- role overload
- role absence
- broken translation between layers
- extraction from the wrong layer
- mismatch between visible authority and actual function

At this point, diagnosis should begin to show where the system is actually held together.

## Step 5: Examine Support Chains

A system survives because support chains exist.

Ask:
- What keeps this system alive?
- What resources does it depend on?
- What trust does it depend on?
- What memory does it borrow?
- What emotional or symbolic investment sustains it?
- Which support chains are stable?
- Which are hidden?
- Which are nearly broken?

A system may look stronger than it is because:
- one person is silently carrying too much
- old trust is still covering new weakness
- the structure is consuming reserves faster than it renews them
- visible order is masking invisible depletion

Support-chain diagnosis is often where hidden fragility becomes visible.

## Step 6: Locate Disturbance, Drift, and Entropy

Once the support chains are visible, identify what is deforming the system.

Look for:
- repeated disturbance
- slow drift
- internal extraction
- rising coordination cost
- ritual without function
- defensive rigidity
- trust erosion
- support chain thinning
- memory loss
- fragmentation pressure

Important distinction:
- disturbance is not the same as disagreement
- drift is not the same as collapse
- entropy is not the same as proof of bad faith

Diagnosis should ask:
- what repeatedly weakens coherence
- what is slowly moving the system away from its functional center
- what is becoming more costly even when no one intends harm

## Step 7: Determine the Stage

Stage judgment changes the meaning of symptoms.

Ask:
- Is the system still forming?
- Is it expanding beyond its current carrying structure?
- Is it hitting a bottleneck?
- Is it in transition?
- Is it stabilizing?
- Is it becoming rigid?
- Is it fragmenting?
- Is it attempting renewal?

A stage diagnosis should remain approximate.
The purpose is not false precision.
The purpose is to avoid shallow interpretation.

The same symptom may mean very different things depending on stage.

## Step 8: Identify the Core Contradiction

The core contradiction is the deepest tension generating multiple visible symptoms.

Ask:
- What visible problems seem to share one center?
- What keeps reproducing the same surface failure?
- What cannot be solved without addressing the deeper tension?

Examples:
- growth outrunning structure
- control outrunning trust
- extraction outrunning renewal
- identity outrunning reality
- speed outrunning memory
- symbolic continuity replacing real function

A diagnosis becomes sharper when the symptoms are gathered around one contradiction rather than treated as unrelated noise.

## Step 9: Set Intervention Priority

A diagnosis becomes useful when it identifies the first repair point with the highest leverage.

Ask:
- What should be repaired first?
- What bottleneck, trust failure, or support-chain break is most decisive?
- What intervention changes the rest if it is repaired?
- What should not be touched first, even if it looks dramatic?

The first repair target is not always:
- the loudest problem
- the most visible conflict
- the most emotionally satisfying target

A disciplined diagnosis looks for structural leverage, not emotional drama.

## Step 10: State Uncertainty

Every serious diagnosis should include uncertainty.

State clearly:
- what seems well supported
- what is inferred rather than directly observed
- what remains unclear
- what alternative interpretation may also fit

Structural analysis becomes dangerous when it pretends to eliminate uncertainty.
A good diagnosis clarifies reality without pretending to exhaust it.

## Final Output Format

A complete diagnosis should ideally end with five parts:

### Structural Judgment
What kind of system is this, and what is happening structurally?

### Core Contradiction
What deep tension is generating the visible symptoms?

### Main Risk Direction
If nothing changes, what kind of drift or failure is most likely?

### Best Intervention Priority
What should be repaired first, and why that point matters most?

### Uncertainty / Alternatives
What remains unclear, and what else might be happening?

## Final Note

A diagnosis should make a system more visible, not more frozen.

Its role is not to dominate reality with concepts.
Its role is to help reality become legible enough for repair.
