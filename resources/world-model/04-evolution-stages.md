# Evolution Stages

This file defines the stage logic used by the world model.

Stages are not destiny.
They are rough structural positions that help interpret symptoms more accurately.

The same visible problem means different things at different stages.

For example:
- conflict in formation is not the same as conflict in fragmentation
- confusion in expansion is not the same as confusion in collapse
- rigidity in stabilization is not the same as rigidity in fear-driven stagnation

The purpose of stage analysis is not to force a system into a fixed narrative.
Its purpose is to improve diagnosis.

## How to use stage analysis

When identifying a stage, ask:

- Is the system still forming?
- Is it expanding beyond its current carrying structure?
- Is it hitting a bottleneck?
- Is it undergoing a transition?
- Is it stable, or merely rigid?
- Is it fragmenting?
- Is it attempting renewal?

A system may contain traces of multiple stages at once.
The goal is to identify the dominant structural condition.
## 1. Formation

Formation is the stage in which the system is still being assembled.

Typical signs:
- roles are still fluid
- the anchor is emerging rather than settled
- trust is being tested
- coordination is improvised
- memory is short
- structure depends heavily on a few active participants

Formation is not failure.
It is a necessary stage of concentration, uncertainty, and pattern emergence.

Common risks in formation:
- premature rigidity
- false clarity
- hidden anchor conflict
- role overload
- fragile dependence on one person

## 2. Expansion

Expansion is the stage in which the system begins to grow beyond its original small structure.

Typical signs:
- more people, functions, or demands enter the system
- the original coordination style begins to strain
- support chains become more important
- translation between layers becomes harder
- success begins to create complexity

Expansion often looks energetic from the outside.
Its danger lies in invisible strain.

Common risks in expansion:
- growth outrunning structure
- communication loss
- memory dilution
- role confusion
- support chains becoming thin

## 3. Bottleneck

Bottleneck is the stage in which the existing structure can no longer process what is being asked of it.

Typical signs:
- repeated delay
- recurring misunderstandings
- key people overloaded
- coordination cost rises sharply
- visible effort increases while effective movement decreases
- people begin solving the same problem again and again

A bottleneck does not mean the system is dead.
It means the system has reached the limit of its current form.

Common risks in bottleneck:
- blaming people for structural strain
- adding more pressure instead of redesign
- pretending increased activity is the same as progress
## 4. Transition

Transition is the stage in which the system is trying, or being forced, to move from one structural form to another.

Typical signs:
- old rules no longer fit
- new rules are not yet trusted
- the anchor may be renegotiated
- some roles lose relevance while others become newly necessary
- instability increases because the old coherence has weakened

Transition is often the most dangerous and most generative stage.

Common risks in transition:
- trying to preserve old form at all cost
- moving too fast without support
- losing institutional memory
- splitting before a new center forms
- confusing structural pain with proof that change is wrong

## 5. Stabilization

Stabilization is the stage in which the system has found a workable pattern and can sustain itself with lower improvisation.

Typical signs:
- roles are clearer
- support chains are more reliable
- coordination is easier
- the anchor is more legible
- repeated tasks become less costly
- memory becomes more durable

Stabilization is a success condition, but not a final one.

Common risks in stabilization:
- mistaking current fit for permanent truth
- becoming blind to emerging entropy
- overprotecting form while underinvesting in renewal
## 6. Rigidity

Rigidity is the stage in which the system still holds form, but has lost flexibility.

Typical signs:
- procedure replaces judgment
- protection of form overrides function
- deviation is treated as threat even when adaptation is needed
- renewal pathways narrow
- legitimacy is maintained symbolically rather than substantively

Rigidity is often mistaken for strength because the system still appears orderly.

Common risks in rigidity:
- suppressing corrective feedback
- confusing obedience with coherence
- punishing change-capable roles
- maintaining visible order while internal vitality declines

## 7. Fragmentation

Fragmentation is the stage in which the system loses enough coherence that parts begin operating as semi-separate realities.

Typical signs:
- anchor split
- trust collapse across layers
- communication channels deform
- local units become self-protective
- common language weakens
- the same system is experienced differently by different parts

Fragmentation does not always look dramatic at first.
It may first appear as low-grade separation, parallel logics, or hidden withdrawal.

Common risks in fragmentation:
- pretending unity still exists because form remains
- trying to fix everything centrally after coherence is already broken
- escalating blame while support chains disappear
## 8. Renewal Attempt

Renewal attempt is the stage in which the system is consciously or unconsciously trying to recover viability.

Typical signs:
- old routines are questioned
- repair priorities become visible
- buried contradictions surface
- support chains are rebuilt or replaced
- new translation pathways appear
- the anchor is either clarified or re-founded

Renewal attempt is not guaranteed success.
It is a structurally significant effort to become livable again.

Common risks in renewal attempt:
- cosmetic reform
- replacing one rigid form with another
- exhausting the system with too much change at once
- failing to protect the few remaining renewal carriers
## Stage Interpretation Rules

### Rule 1: Do not confuse appearance with stage

A system may look stable while actually rigid.
It may look chaotic while actually transitional.
It may look unified while already fragmenting.

### Rule 2: Stage changes symptom meaning

The same symptom changes meaning depending on stage.
Diagnosis without stage awareness is often shallow.

### Rule 3: Systems may overlap stages

One part may be stabilizing while another is fragmenting.
One layer may be renewing while another remains rigid.

### Rule 4: Stage is not destiny

A stage is a structural position, not a final judgment.
A bottleneck may become renewal.
A stabilization may become rigidity.
A fragmentation may still be partially reversed if support chains and anchor can be rebuilt.

## Final Note

Stage analysis matters because it helps distinguish:
- normal formation pain from real breakdown
- healthy adaptation from incoherence
- stability from rigidity
- visible collapse from invisible drift
- real renewal from cosmetic motion
