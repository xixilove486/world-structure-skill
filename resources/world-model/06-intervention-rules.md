# Intervention Rules

This file defines how the world model approaches repair and intervention.

The model is not only for naming structure.
It is also for helping decide what should be repaired first, what should not be touched first, and how to avoid making a damaged system even more brittle.

## General Rule

Intervention should be proportional, staged, and structurally aware.

A bad intervention often does one of three things:
- pushes harder on a system that is already overloaded
- repairs the loudest symptom instead of the highest-leverage break
- destroys what little coherence remains while trying to create something better

## Rule 1: Repair the bottleneck before expanding demand

If a system cannot process what is being asked of it, adding more pressure rarely creates health.

Before demanding more output, ask:
- what is overloaded
- what is failing to translate
- what support chain is thinning
- what hidden cost is being silently absorbed

## Rule 2: Repair trust before increasing control

When trust has weakened, systems often compensate with more control.
This may create visible order, but it often deepens fear, rigidity, and concealment.

Ask:
- is coordination failing because people refuse, or because they no longer trust the structure?
- is the system demanding obedience because it has lost legitimacy?

In many cases, trust repair is the higher-leverage intervention.

## Rule 3: Repair translation between layers

Many systems do not fail because their parts are individually broken.
They fail because layers no longer translate into one another.

Examples:
- decisions no longer become executable action
- frontline reality no longer reaches leadership
- formal process no longer matches informal practice
- identity language no longer matches lived experience

Repairing translation often restores more coherence than adding new rules.

## Rule 4: Protect renewal carriers

In a brittle system, the people or roles still capable of renewal are often overloaded, isolated, distrusted, or punished.

Examples include:
- bridge-builders
- memory holders
- truth carriers
- adaptive coordinators
- emotionally regulating roles
- low-status but high-function contributors

If these carriers collapse or leave, renewal becomes much harder.

## Rule 5: Do not confuse demolition with reform

Sometimes change is necessary.
But not every act of destruction creates renewal.

Ask:
- what coherence will be lost if this is removed?
- what support chain will disappear with it?
- is there a viable replacement, or only anger?
- is this intervention building a new center, or only attacking the old one?

Reform without replacement often accelerates fragmentation.

## Rule 6: Sequence matters

The right intervention in the wrong order can still fail.

Typical good sequence:
1. define the system
2. clarify the anchor
3. identify constraints
4. stabilize the most fragile support chain
5. repair translation or trust
6. only then broaden reform

Sequence should fit stage.
Formation, bottleneck, fragmentation, and renewal attempt do not want the same order.

## Rule 7: Work with the stage, not against it

Different stages require different intervention logic.

Examples:
- formation needs coherence, not overformalization
- expansion needs redesign, not just enthusiasm
- bottleneck needs structural relief, not moral pressure
- fragmentation needs selective re-linking, not slogans of unity
- rigidity needs protected adaptation, not total chaos
- renewal attempt needs pacing and support, not purity tests

## Rule 8: Preserve what still works

Not everything old is dead weight.
Not everything stable is oppression.
Not everything disorderly is renewal.

Good intervention asks:
- what still carries real function
- what still holds trust
- what still transmits memory
- what still links layers
- what should be preserved while repair happens

## Rule 9: Name the likely cost

Every real intervention has cost.

Possible costs include:
- short-term instability
- exposed contradiction
- loss of convenience
- conflict becoming visible
- temporary inefficiency
- grief over old form
- departure of people attached to the old structure

If cost is denied, it returns as resentment or sabotage.

## Rule 10: Stop when coherence returns

The purpose of intervention is not permanent restructuring.
It is to restore enough coherence, trust, carrying capacity, and renewal to let the system live again.

Intervention should not become an identity in itself.

## Final Note

The best intervention is often not the most dramatic one.
It is the one that restores the system’s ability to carry reality without lying, freezing, or devouring itself.
