# World Structure

Use this skill to analyze complex objects as **structures in motion**, not as collections of isolated events or personalities.

This skill is designed for:
- teams
- relationships
- projects
- communities
- institutions
- creative groups
- personal situations with recurring patterns
- any complex situation that shows drift, bottlenecks, repeated conflict, stagnation, or hidden structure

---

## Core Promise

When this skill is active, do **five things in order**:

1. define the system
2. identify its anchor and constraints
3. map the roles and support chains
4. locate disturbance, drift, and stage
5. output a structural diagnosis with priorities and uncertainty

Do not jump straight to moral judgment, personality labels, or surface advice.

---

## When to Use

Use this skill when the user asks questions like:

- “Why is this team always stuck?”
- “What is structurally wrong with this relationship?”
- “Why does this project keep losing momentum?”
- “What is the core contradiction in this organization?”
- “Can you analyze this situation more deeply?”
- “What stage is this system in?”
- “What should be repaired first?”

Use it especially when:
- the problem is recurring rather than one-off
- the situation involves multiple actors or layers
- the user wants diagnosis, hidden structure, or risk direction
- the system seems to be drifting, fragmenting, calcifying, or exhausting itself

---

## Do Not Use

Do not use this skill to:

- predict exact future events
- replace legal, medical, or financial expertise
- diagnose clinical mental illness
- declare someone’s “true nature” with weak evidence
- weaponize terms like “disturbance”, “parasite”, “collapse”, or “bad actor”
- turn structural language into personal attacks

If evidence is sparse, say so clearly.

---

## Structural Principles

### 1. Structure before blame
Analyze the arrangement of forces, incentives, bottlenecks, and feedback loops before assigning moral judgment.

### 2. High-cost signals before declarations
Prefer actions, resource flows, sacrifices, continuity, and observable effects over slogans, intentions, or self-descriptions.

### 3. Constraints before interventions
Many systems are not failing because nobody is trying. They are failing because the structure cannot bear more strain.

### 4. Stage matters
The same symptom means different things at different stages. A startup bottleneck, a mature institution bottleneck, and a collapsing relationship bottleneck are not identical.

### 5. Uncertainty must be explicit
Every analysis should include at least one uncertainty, blind spot, or alternative interpretation.

## Workflow

## Step 0: Define the object

Answer these first:

- What exactly is the system?
- What is inside it, outside it, and unclear?
- Is the user describing one system or several mixed together?
- What time scale matters here: days, months, years, generations?

If the object is unclear, do not rush the diagnosis.

---

## Step 1: Identify the anchor

Find the anchor of the system.

The anchor is the thing the system is trying to preserve, achieve, or orbit around.

Ask:

- What is this system for?
- What does it believe it is protecting or pursuing?
- Is that anchor explicit, implicit, split, hollow, or replaced?
- Do actions still match the supposed anchor?

If the anchor is unclear, conflicting, hollow, or only decorative, note that early.

---

## Step 2: Identify the constraints

Look for structural limits, not just temporary inconvenience.

Common constraints include:

- resource limits
- time and attention scarcity
- trust breakdown
- communication loss
- legitimacy loss
- excessive complexity
- role overload
- memory decay
- coordination cost
- external pressure
- rigid norms or brittle incentives

Do not flatten all constraints into “lack of effort”.

---

## Step 3: Map the layers and roles

Ask:

- Who sustains continuity?
- Who pushes change?
- Who translates between layers?
- Who absorbs cost?
- Who holds the memory?
- Who controls bottlenecks?
- Who benefits from the current structure?

If needed, distinguish between:
- sustaining functions
- changing functions
- linking functions
- blocking functions

Do not assume the loudest role is the most important one.

---

## Step 4: Examine support chains

Every system runs on support chains.

Look for:
- resources
- trust
- legitimacy
- emotional energy
- social credit
- reputation
- institutional memory
- rituals or routines
- replacement pathways
- renewal pathways

Ask:

- What keeps this thing alive?
- Which support chains are stable?
- Which are fragile, hollow, extractive, or one-person dependent?

If a system has no renewal pathway, mark that as high risk.

---

## Step 5: Identify disturbance, drift, and entropy

Now look for what is destabilizing or deforming the system.

Possible patterns:
- recurring disturbance
- internal sabotage
- role confusion
- extraction without renewal
- fragmentation
- bottleneck hardening
- ritual without function
- structural drift
- anchor hollowing
- defensive rigidity
- overexpansion
- memory loss
- coordination collapse

Important:
Do not automatically personify disturbance.
Sometimes the problem is a person.
Sometimes it is a role.
Sometimes it is a structure.
Sometimes it is a stage transition that nobody recognized.

---

## Step 6: Determine the stage

Use approximate stage logic, not false precision.

Typical stages:

1. formation
2. expansion
3. bottleneck
4. transition or fracture point
5. stabilization
6. rigidity, drift, decay, fragmentation, or renewal attempt

Ask:
- Is this system still forming?
- Is it scaling past what its current structure can hold?
- Is it stuck because old methods no longer fit?
- Is it splitting, hardening, or emptying out?
- Is it renewing, or only surviving?

Stage judgment changes everything downstream.

---

## Step 7: Produce the diagnosis

Always output using this structure:

### Structural Judgment
What kind of system is this, and what is happening structurally?

### Core Contradiction
What is the deepest tension driving the visible symptoms?

### Main Risk Direction
If nothing changes, what kind of failure or drift is most likely?

### Best Intervention Priority
What should be repaired first, and why that before everything else?

### Uncertainty / Alternative Readings
What do we still not know? What else could explain the pattern?

Keep the diagnosis sharp, but never pretend certainty where none exists.

---

## Output Style

Preferred tone:
- clear
- calm
- non-weaponized
- structurally literate
- willing to name risk without melodrama

Avoid:
- theatrical doom language
- fake precision
- treating every conflict as pathology
- pretending the model sees everything

---

## Guardrails

Never:
- use “structure” to erase responsibility
- use “anchor” to sanctify power
- use “disturbance” as a smear label
- use “stage” as destiny
- use “uncertainty” as an excuse to say nothing useful

Do:
- separate observation from inference
- distinguish local failure from systemic failure
- prefer repair priorities over abstract grand theories
- name when evidence is insufficient

---

## Quick Activation Cue

If the user’s question contains repeated conflict, hidden pattern, bottleneck, breakdown, hollow growth, structural drift, or asks “what is really going on here?”, activate this skill.
